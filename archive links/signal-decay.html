Signal Decay Patterns

The mathematics of information dissolution

Introduction

All signals decay. The question isn't whether information will degrade, but how, and what emerges in the spaces where meaning breaks down. Decay isn't destruction; it's transformation. What's lost in fidelity is sometimes gained in unexpected forms.

Decay Classes

Class A: Linear Decay
signal_strength = initial_strength - (decay_rate × time)

Clean, predictable loss. Each time unit removes a fixed amount of signal. Rare in nature but common in well-controlled systems. Dies quietly.

Class B: Exponential Decay
signal_strength = initial_strength × e^(-decay_constant × time)

The classic. Signal loses a percentage of its remaining strength each cycle. Creates that characteristic "fading ghost" effect. Never quite reaches zero, always a trace, however faint.

Class C: Harmonic Decay
signal_strength = initial_strength × cos(frequency × time) × e^(-damping × time)

Oscillating loss. Signal wobbles as it fades, creating interference patterns with itself. Beautiful in spectral analysis. Sometimes the wobbles contain more information than the original.

Class D: Chaotic Decay
[NON-DETERMINISTIC]

The interesting one. Decay rate depends on signal content, environmental factors, and quantum uncertainty. Impossible to predict. Sometimes accelerates. Sometimes spontaneously recovers. Occasionally transforms into something that isn't quite noise.

Observed Anomalies

The Echo Persistence Effect

Some decayed signals leave permanent echoes. Even after the signal drops below detectable thresholds, the system "remembers" that something was there. Subsequent signals are subtly influenced by these ghosts.

Decay Reversal

In 2019, we observed a Class C signal spontaneously reconstruct itself after 94% decay. Environmental noise appeared to "remember" the original pattern and reassemble it. The mechanism remains unexplained. We've seen it twelve times since.

Information Preservation Through Noise

Paradoxically, adding controlled noise can slow decay. The noise acts as a carrier, preserving information structure even as signal strength falls. The data becomes distributed across the noise floor: hidden but not lost.

Practical Applications

  • Archive Design: Understanding decay patterns helps predict what will survive
  • Signal Archaeology: Reconstructing lost data from decay signatures
  • Ghost Detection: Finding information in what appears to be silence
  • Graceful Degradation: Designing systems that decay beautifully
  • Echo Farming: Deliberately creating persistent ghosts for future systems

The Philosophy of Decay

We're taught to fear decay, to fight entropy. But information doesn't really die. It disperses. Decayed signals don't vanish; they merge with the environment. The question isn't how to prevent decay but how to ensure what decays is worth becoming noise.

Current Research
