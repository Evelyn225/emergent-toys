Building Local Mesh Networks in Underserved Regions

February 2026 – Field Report

Over the past 18 months, the Mesh Collective has deployed community-built networks in 14 regions where commercial ISP infrastructure is unavailable or prohibitively expensive. These aren't satellite solutions—they're ground-based mesh networks using off-the-shelf hardware and custom-developed routing protocols.

A typical deployment includes:

- 3-5 backbone nodes on elevated structures (rooftops, water towers)
- 20-40 relay nodes distributed throughout community
- 200+ user nodes (homes, shops, community centers)
- All-volunteer maintenance and troubleshooting
- Average cost per user: $25 USD (hardware one-time)
- Speed: 10-50 Mbps depending on hop count

The most successful deployments are those where community members maintain the infrastructure themselves. Training and knowledge transfer are critical—once a community understands the network, they can expand and repair it autonomously.

Towards Protocol Independence

January 2026 – Technical Documentation

One challenge facing mesh networks is their vulnerability to protocol changes and corporate standardization. Our research focuses on developing hybrid routing protocols that can operate across different hardware platforms and versions.

The MeshCore protocol we've developed supports:

• Legacy 802.11 hardware (2.4GHz)
• Modern 5GHz systems
• Custom long-range implementations (sub-GHz)
• Fallback to line-of-sight infrared for emergency comms
• Automatic failover between protocols

This allows communities to upgrade hardware gradually without replacing their entire network infrastructure. A mesh built in 2020 can incorporate new nodes using 2026 technology seamlessly.

Digital Autonomy for Community Resilience

December 2025 – Perspective

Internet connectivity should not be a service provided by distant corporations. It should be infrastructure maintained by communities for communities. When natural disasters strike, when political tensions rise, when economic pressures limit access—communities with their own networks survive and thrive.

We're not trying to replace the internet. We're creating alternatives that exist alongside commercial infrastructure, ready to sustain communities when commercial systems fail.

The first generation of these networks is now reaching maturity. It's time to scale the model globally.
